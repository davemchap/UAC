              About the Forecast Usability Scorecard

              What is this app?

              The Forecast Usability Scorecard evaluates avalanche forecasts for how well different types of backcountry users can read, understand, and act on them. Each tab is a separate prototype solution exploring a different angle of forecast usability.

              The 8 Solutions

              ◕ Readability Lens
              Scores each forecast for clarity, jargon load, and actionability across all personas. Colored underlines show phrases that may confuse a specific reader. Click persona chips to toggle highlights on/off.
              ★ Forecaster Coach
              Translates persona scores into writing feedback for forecasters. The grade card shows overall performance; coaching suggestions offer specific rewrites. Uses the worst-outcome callout to flag the most dangerous misread.
              🔍 Knowledge Gaps
              Inventories the technical concepts a forecast assumes readers know (e.g. "wind slab", "aspect"). The Knowledge Gap Matrix maps each persona against each concept — orange/red cells mean that reader likely missed that concept entirely.
              👤 What They Heard
              Simulates how each persona interpreted each section of the forecast. Comprehension levels: HIGH = understood correctly, MEDIUM = partial understanding, LOW = missed key points, MISREAD = drew the wrong conclusion.
              🎯 Decision Check
              Predicts what decision each persona made (go/caution/no-go) and whether it matches the forecast's intent. INVERTED = the forecast communicated the opposite of its intended message to that reader.
              📅 Daily Report
              An aggregated summary for all zones on a given day — shows which zones had the worst comprehension, which personas struggled most, and where assumption density was highest. Requires live scoring data.
              📈 Weekly Report
              Aggregated quality trends over a calendar week, broken down by forecaster and zone. Shows improving/declining/stable trends vs. the prior 3-week average. Requires at least one week of live data.
              ⚡ Pre-Forecast Review
              Pre-publication draft review for forecasters. Paste or upload a draft forecast before publishing and get instant persona-based feedback: readability score, knowledge gaps, predicted decision accuracy, and prioritized coaching notes — all synthesized into one glanceable view optimized for the pre-dawn publishing window.

              The Persona Builder (⚙ Train Personas)

              The Train Personas button opens the persona builder. Each persona represents a type of backcountry user. You can edit their background, add behavioral rules, and test-drive how they respond. Active built-in personas drive all scoring.

              How Persona Attributes Affect Scoring

              Each persona has attributes that directly change how forecasts are scored. Here is what each one does:

              AttributeRangeHow it affects scoring
              Avalanche Training Level 0 – 5 Determines which jargon terms are recognized. Level 0 (no training) = UAC technical terms score as 0. Level 3+ (AIARE 2) = most UAC jargon is understood. The single strongest driver of jargon score.
              Risk Tolerance 1 – 5 High tolerance (4–5) = persona needs explicit go/no-go language or they default to go. Low tolerance (1–2) = a general warning triggers caution without a specific action cue. Drives actionability score and Decision Mirror.
              Terrain Assessment Skill 1 – 5 Affects comprehension of aspect, elevation band, and terrain-feature language. Low skill = "E-facing slopes above 9,000 ft" is meaningless. High skill = that phrase is a precise, actionable signal.
              Years of Mountain Experience 0 – 40+ Baseline experience modifier for general mountain conditions language. Experienced users recognize seasonal snowpack references that novices skip over.
              Backcountry Days per Season 0 – 100+ Frequency modifier — heavy users (30+ days) have high familiarity with snowpack language and seasonal patterns. Occasional users (1–5 days) process the same terms more slowly and with less context.
              Local Terrain Familiarity 1 – 5 Affects comprehension of zone-specific place names, local hazard indicators, and named terrain features. Low familiarity = place names add noise rather than signal.
              Group Decision Tendency solo / group / leader Used in the Decision Mirror to simulate whether risk is amplified (group pressure) or dampened (solo caution). Groups with a risk-tolerant leader tend to normalize danger ratings downward.
              Weather Pattern Recognition 1 – 5 Affects comprehension of wind, temperature, and weather-driven hazard descriptions. Low recognition = wind slab formation language is abstract. High recognition = it is a concrete, actionable warning.
