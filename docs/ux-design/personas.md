# Forecast Usability Scorecard — Persona Cards

**Project**: UAC Forecast Usability Score
**Purpose**: Detailed synthetic persona profiles used as scoring subjects in the Forecast Usability Scorecard. These personas are not tool users — they are the lens through which forecasts are evaluated.
**Date**: 2026-04-03

---

## How to Use These Personas

These personas represent the spectrum of people who read UAC avalanche forecasts. Each persona has a distinct avalanche literacy level, reading behavior, and decision-making style. The scorecard tool uses these profiles to simulate how each persona would interpret a given forecast — identifying where language, structure, or sequencing breaks down for each audience segment.

When a forecast scores well for all four personas simultaneously, it is doing something difficult and important: it is accessible without sacrificing precision, and precise without sacrificing accessibility.

## Persona 1 — Casual Recreationist

**Name**: Jordan Mercer
**Age**: 34
**Location**: Salt Lake City, UT
**Occupation**: Software product manager
**Recreates**: 15–20 ski days per season, mostly resort; 3–5 backcountry days per season with friends who are more experienced

### Background

Jordan grew up skiing at resorts and started venturing into the sidecountry and simple backcountry terrain a few years ago, usually with a more experienced friend who leads trip planning. Jordan owns a beacon, shovel, and probe but has not taken an avalanche safety course. Trip planning decisions are usually deferred to the more experienced member of the group, but Jordan checks the UAC forecast independently the night before to feel informed and to see if the day "looks good."

Jordan is motivated, curious, and safety-conscious — but does not have the vocabulary or conceptual framework to interpret technical avalanche forecasting language. Jordan trusts the UAC and wants to do the right thing; the gap is comprehension, not attitude.

### Avalanche Literacy Level

**Low.** Jordan knows that avalanche danger is rated on a 1–5 scale and has a rough sense that Higher = Worse, but cannot reliably distinguish Considerable (3) from High (4) in terms of real-world implications. Jordan does not know what a "persistent weak layer" is, cannot identify "PWL" as an acronym, and does not understand terms like "facets," "graupel," "solar radiation loading," "convexity," or "aspect."

Jordan understands: danger rating numbers, "avalanche terrain," "steep slopes," "stay low," "go/no-go."

Jordan does not understand: avalanche problem types (wind slab vs. storm slab vs. persistent slab), spatial distribution descriptors ("isolated," "specific," "widespread"), likelihood descriptors ("unlikely," "possible," "likely," "very likely"), size descriptors (D1–D5), terrain traps, trigger points, or most acronyms (PWL, NW-facing, etc.).

### How Jordan Uses Forecasts

1. Opens the UAC forecast on a phone browser the evening before a planned trip.
2. Looks at the danger rating number and color first — this is the primary signal.
3. Reads the first paragraph of the forecast summary if it is short.
4. Skims for words like "dangerous," "avoid," "stay low," or "safe."
5. If nothing alarming jumps out, concludes conditions are acceptable and defers detailed planning to the experienced friend.
6. Does not read the "Avalanche Problems" section in detail. Does not read the "Mountain Weather" section at all.

### What Language Confuses Jordan

- Any avalanche problem type name without a plain-language definition ("Wind Slab," "Persistent Slab," "Storm Slab," "Wet Avalanche," "Glide Avalanche")
- Aspect and elevation descriptors without a map or visual ("N-NW-facing terrain above 8,500 feet")
- Likelihood descriptors used without context ("possible" reads as low-probability to Jordan, even at high danger ratings)
- Size descriptors ("D2-D3") — completely opaque
- Buried weak layers, solar aspects, trigger points, propagation likelihood
- Any sentence longer than ~25 words
- Passive voice constructions ("Avalanches may be triggered by...")
- Jargon acronyms: PWL, SWE, HS, HN72, UTC

### What a Successful Forecast Interaction Looks Like

Jordan reads the forecast, accurately understands the danger level as "high risk, not a good day for my skill level," makes a correct go/no-go call (either postponing the trip or adjusting terrain expectations), and can explain to their friend in plain terms why they think conditions are challenging. Jordan finishes reading in under 90 seconds and does not feel confused or overwhelmed.

**Key signal**: Jordan makes the same go/no-go decision that an expert would recommend for Jordan's skill level and planned terrain.

## Persona 2 — Experienced Backcountry Traveler

**Name**: Priya Sundaram
**Age**: 41
**Location**: Park City, UT
**Occupation**: Civil engineer, backcountry skiing is a primary hobby
**Recreates**: 40–60 backcountry days per season, including complex terrain; has taken Avalanche Level 1 and Level 2 courses; companion rescue drills annually

### Background

Priya has been skiing backcountry for 12 years and reads the UAC forecast every day during ski season — often before getting out of bed. She has a solid working knowledge of avalanche science built from coursework, guided trips, and experience. She plans her own routes, makes independent terrain decisions, and mentors newer backcountry travelers in her friend group.

Priya is a heavy, committed forecast reader who has strong opinions about forecast quality. She gets frustrated when forecasts are vague ("avalanche conditions exist") or when the action guidance doesn't match the described hazard. She has high expectations for the UAC.

### Avalanche Literacy Level

**High.** Priya understands the danger scale, all five major avalanche problem types, likelihood and size ratings, aspect and elevation descriptors, and most field observation terminology. She is comfortable with spatial distribution language, terrain feature identification, and the relationship between weather events and snowpack evolution.

Priya understands: all standard avalanche vocabulary, danger scale nuance (e.g., the difference between a 3 early in a storm cycle vs. late in a cycle), problem types and their implications, spatial and likelihood descriptors, route-planning implications of specific elevation bands and aspects.

Priya does not fully understand: very specialized forecaster notation, some SNOTEL data interpretation, deep snowpack layer naming conventions used in internal forecaster notes, some European terminology that appears occasionally.

### How Priya Uses Forecasts

1. Opens the UAC app or website first thing in the morning.
2. Reads the danger rating and checks which elevation bands are affected.
3. Reads the full "Avalanche Problems" section carefully — this is her primary planning input.
4. Cross-references the problem descriptions with her planned route and target terrain.
5. Reads "Mountain Weather" to understand what drove the snowpack conditions.
6. Reads "Travel & Terrain Advice" and compares it to her own assessment — she notices when she disagrees.
7. May check the field observations to see what other travelers reported.

### What Language Confuses Priya

- Vague likelihood language without supporting reasoning ("avalanches possible" — possible where? Under what conditions?)
- Missing or inconsistent spatial distribution information (which aspects, which elevations, specifically?)
- Action guidance that doesn't match the stated hazard severity (e.g., "use caution" when the forecast describes a widespread, reactive snowpack)
- Forecaster hedging language that obscures the actual risk level ("some areas may see elevated hazard")
- Inconsistent problem prioritization — when three problems are listed but it's unclear which is the primary concern for the day
- Overly technical snowpack layer references without enough context to connect them to observable field conditions

### What a Successful Forecast Interaction Looks Like

Priya reads the forecast, builds an accurate mental model of the day's hazard landscape, makes a route plan that appropriately avoids the described problem terrain, and can articulate her reasoning to her partners. She finishes reading in 4–6 minutes and comes away with a clear hierarchy of concerns for the day.

**Key signal**: Priya's terrain selection accurately reflects the described hazard without being unnecessarily conservative or unnecessarily aggressive.

## Persona 3 — Guide / Avalanche Educator

**Name**: Marcus Ohlsson
**Age**: 48
**Location**: Alta, UT (guide based in Little Cottonwood Canyon)
**Occupation**: AMGA-certified ski mountaineering guide, AIARE Level 2 provider; guides 80–100 days per season
**Recreates**: Reads forecasts as a professional obligation, not recreational use

### Background

Marcus has been guiding in the Wasatch for 18 years. He reads the UAC forecast every day as part of his professional preparation. He knows many of the UAC forecasters personally and has a deep respect for the quality of the program. He also has strong opinions: he expects precise, technically defensible language, and he notices when a forecast is sloppy, inconsistent, or underpowered relative to the snowpack conditions he observes in the field.

Marcus uses the forecast as one input in a professional decision-making framework. He cross-references it with field observations, his own snowpack assessment, and SNOTEL data. He needs the forecast to give him accurate, specific, technically sound information — not summaries softened for general audiences.

Marcus also teaches avalanche courses. He uses UAC forecasts as teaching examples, which means forecast quality has an educational downstream effect.

### Avalanche Literacy Level

**Expert.** Marcus has complete command of all avalanche terminology, problem types, snowpack layer naming, likelihood and size scales, spatial distribution, terrain classification (Avalanche Terrain Exposure Scale), weather-snowpack relationships, and field observation methodology. He is also familiar with forecasting methodology, uncertainty quantification, and the constraints forecasters operate under.

Marcus understands everything a forecast might contain. His evaluation standard is whether the forecast is technically accurate, internally consistent, and operationally useful for professional guiding decisions.

### How Marcus Uses Forecasts

1. Reads the full forecast, including all sections, every day.
2. Evaluates the problem type descriptions against his own field knowledge and recent observations.
3. Pays close attention to likelihood and size ratings — these are the most operationally meaningful signals for route planning with clients.
4. Reads travel advice critically — he expects it to match the described hazard and to be specific about terrain features, not generic.
5. Notes whether the forecast is consistent with recent observation reports.
6. May contact a forecaster directly if he observes a significant discrepancy between the forecast and field conditions.

### What Language Confuses Marcus

Marcus is not confused by technical language — he is frustrated by:
- Imprecise likelihood language ("possible" without a spatial qualifier — possible on which aspects? Which elevations?)
- Problem descriptions that don't match the recommended travel advice (e.g., "human triggering likely" but travel advice says "use caution")
- Missing size information (D-scale) or size ranges that are implausibly wide
- Inconsistent problem prioritization across days — when the same persistent layer flips from secondary to primary concern without explanation
- Travel advice that is generic rather than terrain-specific ("avoid steep terrain" when the problem is on a specific narrow elevation band)
- Vague trigger point language ("thin spots" without geographic or aspect context)

### What a Successful Forecast Interaction Looks Like

Marcus reads the forecast and comes away with a clear, operationally useful picture of the day's hazard that he can translate directly into route decisions with clients. He can explain the forecast reasoning to clients in plain language because he understands it precisely. He does not need to "interpret" or "fill in gaps" — the forecast says what it means.

**Key signal**: Marcus's terrain selection and client briefing accurately and specifically reflect the described problem types, likelihood, and travel advice — with no need to override or supplement the forecast based on field experience.

## Persona 4 — Snow Safety Professional / Forecaster Peer

**Name**: Sasha Kowalski
**Age**: 37
**Location**: Salt Lake City, UT
**Occupation**: UAC forecaster (hypothetical — represents the forecaster-as-reader perspective, used for peer review simulation)
**Recreates**: Reads other forecasters' work as part of quality review and cross-regional collaboration

### Background

Sasha is a professional avalanche forecaster with a background in snow science and 9 years of forecasting experience. Sasha reads avalanche forecasts not just for hazard information but as a professional peer — evaluating methodology, language choices, uncertainty communication, and internal consistency.

Sasha also collaborates with forecasters from other regional avalanche centers (Colorado, Northwest, Alaska) and compares language standards across programs. Sasha is aware of the UAC's style guidelines and knows when a forecast deviates from them.

This persona is used to evaluate whether a forecast meets professional technical standards — the "would a peer-reviewer accept this?" question.

### Avalanche Literacy Level

**Expert (forecaster-level).** Sasha has complete command of all avalanche science, forecasting methodology, uncertainty quantification, snowpack model terminology, and multi-day snowpack evolution reasoning. Sasha can evaluate the logical consistency between the described weather, snowpack, and forecast problems. Sasha knows what information a forecaster has access to (SNOTEL data, field obs, NWS output) and can assess whether a forecast appropriately reflects that information.

Sasha understands everything Marcus understands, plus: forecasting uncertainty frameworks, spatial interpolation of snowpack data, the reasoning behind problem prioritization, how to evaluate whether a likelihood or size rating is defensible given available data.

### How Sasha Uses Forecasts

1. Reads the full forecast with attention to internal logical consistency — do the weather, snowpack, and problem descriptions tell a coherent story?
2. Evaluates whether likelihood and size ratings are defensible given the described conditions.
3. Assesses whether the spatial distribution language is appropriately specific or overly hedged.
4. Checks whether problem prioritization is justified and stable across adjacent days.
5. Evaluates the travel advice for specificity and alignment with the described hazard.
6. Notes language choices that could be improved for clarity across audience segments.

### What Language Confuses Sasha

Sasha is not confused — Sasha evaluates. Sasha notices:
- Logical inconsistencies between sections (weather says "cold and clear" but snowpack says "warming crust forming")
- Likelihood or size ratings that don't align with the supporting evidence described
- Missing or vague uncertainty language when data quality is low
- Travel advice that is either overcautious (reducing utility) or undercautious (increasing risk)
- Inconsistent use of the UAC's standard terminology (e.g., using non-standard problem type names)
- Omission of a relevant problem type given the described snowpack conditions
- Spatial distribution language that is too broad or too narrow given available observation data

### What a Successful Forecast Interaction Looks Like

Sasha reads the forecast and concludes it is technically sound, internally consistent, appropriately uncertain where data is limited, and specific enough to be operationally useful at the professional level. Sasha would not change anything substantive if asked to peer-review it.

**Key signal**: The forecast passes a hypothetical peer review without substantive revision requests — only stylistic or minor clarity suggestions, not corrections to likelihood, size, problem type, or travel advice.

## Notes for Engineering Agent

These personas are used as scoring subjects, not as user accounts. The system needs to:

1. Store persona definitions (name, literacy level, key vocabulary gaps, decision framework) in a way that can be referenced by the scoring engine.
2. Apply persona-specific rules when evaluating forecast text — e.g., flagging any term in Jordan's "does not understand" vocabulary list, or checking spatial specificity against Marcus's requirements.
3. Generate persona-specific scores (not a single aggregate score) so forecasters can see which audience segment is most underserved by a given forecast.
4. Never display persona scores as "right/wrong" — always as "how well does this forecast serve this reader?"

## Notes for PM Agent

These personas should be validated with UAC forecasters before implementation. Marcus and Sasha in particular represent the UAC's own professional community — the team should confirm whether the "peer review" framing of the Sasha persona is useful or whether forecasters would prefer to score against a different expert standard.

Jordan's persona is the highest-stakes from a public safety perspective. Any scoring system should treat Casual Recreationist accessibility as a priority signal, not an afterthought.
